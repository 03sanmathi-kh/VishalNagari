Module 1: Adaptive Resource Flow in EV Battery Manufacturing

Problem Statement

Vishalanagari’s EV industry depends heavily on lithium, cobalt, and rare earth metals for battery production. However, the arrival of these raw materials is highly volatile due to global supply chain disruptions, mining delays, and transport bottlenecks. Factories often face uneven distribution, leading to idle production lines or inefficient overstocking. The challenge is to dynamically allocate fluctuating material arrivals across multiple factories to minimize production delays, maximize throughput, and ensure fair distribution. A real-time adaptive system is needed to balance urgency, efficiency, and sustainability in resource allocation.

Algorithms Used

1. Greedy Allocation: Quickly assigns incoming materials to the most urgent and efficient factories. Ensures no material sits idle and avoids immediate production delays.

2. Dynamic Programming (DP): Considers multiple time steps and factory states to maximize overall throughput. Stores subproblem results to avoid recomputation, balancing fairness and efficiency across the EV ecosystem.

3. Priority Queues: Factories are ranked dynamically based on urgency (idle lines, backlog orders) and efficiency (output per unit of material). This allows the system to always pick the best candidate factory in logarithmic time.

4. Sliding Window Forecasting: Smooths recent arrival data to predict short-term availability. Helps decide whether to allocate immediately or reserve stock for upcoming high-priority demand.

5. Feedback Loop (Reinforcement Learning): Monitors outcomes after each allocation cycle and adjusts factory scores automatically. The system learns from past performance, becoming adaptive and self-improving over time.

Algorithm Efficiency

  • Greedy Allocation: O(n log n) using priority queues. Fast for up to 100 factories, ensures immediate responsiveness.
  • Dynamic Programming: O(n × T) where T is forecast horizon. Provides optimal throughput planning across multiple time steps.
  • Queue Updates: O(n) lightweight recalculations to refresh factory rankings after each allocation cycle.
  • Forecasting: O(m) where m = number of material types. Efficient for short-term predictions of volatile supply chains.
  • Feedback Loop: O(k) per learning episode, where k = number of actions. Adds adaptability without heavy computational cost.

Overall: The hybrid system achieves real-time responsiveness (Greedy) while maintaining long-term optimality (DP). It scales well for Vishalanagari’s EV industry, balancing speed, fairness, and sustainability.
